What is a Stirling Engine?

A Stirling engine is an external combustion engine that converts heat energy into mechanical work through cyclic compression and expansion of a working gas (air). Unlike internal combustion engines, the heat source is external — making it able to run on any heat source including solar, waste heat, or a flame. The engine in this unit uses an electric heater to supply heat to one end, while the other end is cooled by the environment.

The Thermodynamic Cycle

The ideal Stirling cycle consists of four processes. You can observe each phase in the live P-V diagram on the Main App tab.

1

Isothermal Expansion

The gas expands at constant temperature, absorbing heat from the hot source. Volume increases, pressure decreases.

2

Isochoric Cooling

Gas passes through the regenerator, depositing heat. Volume stays constant; temperature and pressure fall.

3

Isothermal Compression

Gas is compressed at constant temperature, rejecting heat to the cold sink. Volume decreases, pressure rises.

4

Isochoric Heating

Gas absorbs heat stored in the regenerator at constant volume. Temperature and pressure increase without work.

Reading the Live P-V Diagram

The P-V (Pressure–Volume) diagram plots gas pressure on the Y-axis against cylinder volume on the X-axis in real time. The area enclosed by one cycle loop equals the net work output of the engine — displayed as the "Work" value in the chart corner. A larger, rounder loop means more work per cycle. Watch how changing the heater setpoint and load affects the shape.

Tip: Switch to the Main App tab and raise the heater setpoint to 60°C to see a well-developed P-V loop.

Controls Guide

Heater ON/OFF

Enables or disables the heater element. The elapsed time counter tracks total heater-on time for your experiment log.

Heater Setpoint

Target heater temperature (20–70°C). The hardware uses an on/off controller to maintain this temperature.

Load Control

Applies electrical load (0–100%) to the engine's generator, changing the mechanical resistance. Higher load = slower RPM = more torque.

Sweep

Automatically ramps load from 0% to 100% in configurable steps and intervals — useful for generating a load-response curve.

CSV Save

Records all sensor data (pressure, volume, RPM, temperature) with timestamps to a CSV file for analysis in Excel or Python.

Experiment Procedure

1

Connect the Hardware

Connect the USB cable. The status banner should change from OFFLINE to ONLINE. The unit auto-detects by VID/PID.

2

Set Heater Setpoint

Use the slider or input to set a temperature (e.g., 50°C). Click Heater ON and wait 2–3 minutes for the engine to reach steady state.

3

Start CSV Recording

Click Start CSV Save and choose a filename. The button turns green while recording.

4

Run the Load Sweep

Set Sweep Step to 5% and Interval to 500ms. Click Start Sweep. Observe how RPM, pressure, and work change as load increases.

5

Record Results

Stop CSV Save when the sweep completes. Open the CSV in Excel. Plot RPM vs Load and Work vs Load to find the maximum power point.
