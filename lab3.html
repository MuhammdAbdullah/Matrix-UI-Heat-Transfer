🧱 Lab 3: Thermal Resistance

Understanding Resistance to Heat Flow

📚 What is Thermal Resistance?

Thermal resistance (R) is a material's ability to resist heat flow. It's like electrical resistance, but for heat instead of electricity! Just as a resistor slows down electric current, thermal resistance slows down heat flow.

💡 Think of It Like Water Flow:

Imagine water flowing through pipes. A narrow pipe (high resistance) slows the flow, while a wide pipe (low resistance) allows fast flow. Similarly, insulation has high thermal resistance (blocks heat), while metals have low thermal resistance (conduct heat easily).

Thermal Resistance: R = d / (k × A)

Or using temperature and heat flow:
R = ΔT / Q

Where:
• R = Thermal resistance (K/W or °C/W)
• d = Thickness (m)
• k = Thermal conductivity (W/m·K)
• A = Area (m²)
• ΔT = Temperature difference (K or °C)
• Q = Heat flow rate (W)

🎯 Key Concepts

Relationship to Conductivity

Thermal resistance is the inverse of thermal conductance. Materials with high thermal conductivity (like copper) have LOW thermal resistance. Materials with low thermal conductivity (like foam insulation) have HIGH thermal resistance.

Important Facts:

  • Higher R-value: Better insulation (blocks more heat)
  • Lower R-value: Worse insulation (allows more heat)
  • Thicker materials: Higher resistance (more to get through)
  • Larger area: Lower resistance (more paths for heat)

R-Values in Building Construction

In construction, insulation is rated by R-value. For example:

  • R-13: Standard wall insulation
  • R-30: Typical attic insulation
  • R-49: High-efficiency attic insulation
  • Higher numbers = Better insulation!

⚡ Thermal Circuits: Series and Parallel

Series Resistance (Layers)

When heat flows through multiple layers (like a wall with drywall, insulation, and siding), the resistances ADD UP, just like series resistors in an electrical circuit.

R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ... + Rₙ

Example: Wall with 3 Layers

🧱 Brick → 🧴 Insulation → 📋 Drywall

R_total = R_brick + R_insulation + R_drywall

Parallel Resistance (Side-by-Side Paths)

When heat can flow through multiple parallel paths, thermal resistances combine like parallel electrical resistors:

1/R_total = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ... + 1/Rₙ

🔬 Thermal Resistance in Your Apparatus

Understanding Resistance in Your Experiment:

  • Metal bar: Low thermal resistance → Heat conducts easily along the bar
  • Air around bar: High thermal resistance → Insulates the bar
  • With fan ON: Lower convective resistance → More heat removed
  • With fan OFF: Higher convective resistance → Less heat removed
  • Temperature drop: Larger drop = Higher resistance in that section

By measuring temperatures at different points (T1-T8), you can calculate the thermal resistance of each section of the bar. Sections with bigger temperature drops have higher resistance!
