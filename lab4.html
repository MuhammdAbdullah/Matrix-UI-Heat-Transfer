💨 Lab 4: Forced vs Natural Convection

Understanding How Air Movement Affects Heat Transfer

📚 What is Convection?

Convection is heat transfer by the movement of fluids (liquids or gases). When air or water moves over a hot surface, it carries heat away. The faster the fluid moves, the more heat it can remove!

There are two main types of convection:

🌊 Natural Convection

Fluid movement caused by density differences due to temperature changes. Hot air rises, cold air sinks - no fans needed!

Examples:

  • Heat from a radiator
  • Boiling water bubbles
  • Hot air balloon rising
  • Warm air rising in a room

💨 Forced Convection

Fluid movement caused by external means like fans, pumps, or wind. Much faster and more effective!

Examples:

  • Computer cooling fans
  • Car radiator with fan
  • Hair dryer
  • Air conditioning
Convection Heat Transfer: Q = h × A × ΔT

Where:
• Q = Heat transfer rate (W)
• h = Convection coefficient (W/m²·K)
• A = Surface area (m²)
• ΔT = Temperature difference between surface and fluid (K or °C)

⚖️ Comparing Natural and Forced Convection

Characteristic Natural Convection Forced Convection
Driving Force Buoyancy (density differences) External (fan, pump)
Speed Slow (< 1 m/s) Fast (1-100+ m/s)
h coefficient Low (5-25 W/m²·K) High (25-250+ W/m²·K)
Heat Transfer Rate Lower Much Higher
Energy Cost Free (no power needed) Requires power
Control Limited control Easy to control (fan speed)

Key Insight:

  • Forced convection can be 10-20 times more effective than natural convection!
  • This is why computers have fans - natural convection alone isn't enough!

🔬 Observing Convection in Your Apparatus

What Your Fan Does:

  • Fan OFF (0%): Natural convection only - slow cooling
  • Fan ON (25-50%): Forced convection - moderate cooling
  • Fan ON (75-100%): Strong forced convection - rapid cooling

Observable Effects:

1. Temperature Drop: Higher fan speed → Lower temperatures
2. Cooling Rate: Faster fan → Faster temperature decrease
3. Temperature Gradient: More airflow → Steeper gradients
4. Steady-State: Fan controls final equilibrium temperature

Why Does This Matter?

Understanding convection is crucial for:

  • Electronics Cooling: CPUs and GPUs need forced convection
  • HVAC Systems: Moving air distributes heating/cooling
  • Industrial Processes: Cooling hot materials efficiently
  • Energy Efficiency: Natural convection saves power when possible

🧪 Experimental Procedure

Step-by-Step Experiment to Compare Convection Types

📋 Setup:

  1. Set heater to 70°C
  2. Wait for steady-state (temperatures stop changing)
  3. Record all temperatures (T1-T8)

🌊 Part 1: Natural Convection

  1. Set fan to 0% (OFF)
  2. Wait 5-10 minutes for steady-state
  3. Record all T1-T8 temperatures
  4. Note how long it took to stabilize

💨 Part 2: Forced Convection - Low Speed

  1. Set fan to 25%
  2. Wait for new steady-state
  3. Record all T1-T8 temperatures
  4. Compare to natural convection

💨 Part 3: Forced Convection - High Speed

  1. Set fan to 75%
  2. Wait for new steady-state
  3. Record all T1-T8 temperatures
  4. Compare to previous cases

📊 Analysis:

  • Plot Temperature vs Distance for each fan speed
  • Calculate average temperature drop for each case
  • Estimate convection coefficients
  • Calculate improvement factor (forced vs natural)
